# Introduction

Marketers invest significant effort, time, and resources into developing strategies to grow their brands, launch new products, and execute advertising, pricing promotions, and public relations (PR) campaigns.

Despite these efforts, marketers often expect that such investments will result in loyal, emotionally connected customers. However, the reality is quite different. Most people are too occupied with everyday life to form deep attachments to brands. Instead, they make quick decisions—often based on convenience or habit. Even brands they have previously liked can be forgotten, and consumer preferences can change rapidly.

So, what is the purpose of all this effort? Why do marketers persist?

In this chapter, you will explore key concepts such as loyalty, attitudes, memory, and motivation, along with the importance of mental and physical availability. Understanding buyer behaviour is essential for making effective marketing decisions.

# Knowledge is Power

As a marketer, you are expected to have a deeper understanding of your company’s customers than anyone else in the organisation. This does not mean having an in-depth psychological profile of each individual customer—that would be unrealistic. Instead, your understanding will gradually improve as you learn how various factors influence buying behaviour.

Many daily marketing decisions rely on answering fundamental questions:

* **Who buys?**
* **Where do they buy?**
* **When do they buy?**
* **How much do they buy?**

It is essential to track descriptive data about both current and potential customers, including:

* What they buy
* What they watch
* How your brand fits into their lives

However, data alone is not enough. It must be interpreted. Skilled marketers analyse data using established, law-like patterns that help identify trends and anomalies. These patterns apply across various contexts, including:

* Industrial purchases (e.g., concrete, aviation fuel)
* Retail brand purchases
* Store selection behaviour

While this chapter does not explore these patterns in detail, it focuses on what they reveal about buying behaviour and brand attitudes.

## The Limits of Intuition

Traditional marketers often rely on intuition and introspection, assuming their own behaviours and preferences reflect those of their customers. This approach is flawed.

Marketing professionals typically differ significantly from their customers—they are often better educated, earn higher incomes, and have a stronger emotional connection to the brand because it is tied to their livelihood. As a result, their perspective is not representative of the average consumer.

This is why **market research is critical**. Relying solely on intuition or internal opinions is insufficient (see Chapter 4 on Market Research).

---

## The Role of Market Research

Marketers benefit from a well-established market research industry, including global firms such as **Nielsen** and **Kantar**, as well as numerous specialised local agencies.

These organisations collect data through surveys that capture:

* Consumer behaviours (e.g., watching, shopping, buying, consuming)
* Consumer perceptions and opinions

Marketers must interpret this data using established knowledge of buying behaviour and human psychology. This chapter introduces some of these foundational principles.

# Most Buying is Repeat Buying

A typical supermarket carries around **40,000 products**, making even simple shopping decisions seem overwhelming. Similarly, consumers face vast choices in other areas:

* Hundreds of financial institutions
* Thousands of accountancy firms
* Numerous hairdressers
* Hundreds of television channels

Given this abundance of choice, how do consumers manage?

The answer is simple: **most buying behaviour is repetitive**.

Consumers typically:

* Purchase from categories they are already familiar with
* Choose brands they have bought before
* Shop at stores they have previously visited or that are similar

This makes consumers experienced, though not necessarily experts. Most people do not aim to become experts—they simply want to make satisfactory decisions without investing excessive time or effort.

---

## Habit and Convenience Drive Behaviour

Many marketing textbooks overemphasise rational decision-making and deliberate evaluation. While such behaviour does occur, it is relatively rare.

In reality, most purchasing decisions are driven by:

* **Habit**
* **Convenience**

Consumers develop a **repertoire**—a stable set of preferred brands or stores—and repeatedly choose from this limited selection.

---

## Repertoire Buying and Loyalty

This chapter explores the concept of **repertoire buying**, which can be understood as loyalty to a limited number of brands.

Key considerations include:

* How marketers can **encourage loyalty** to their brand
* How to **disrupt or influence** existing loyalties toward competing brands

Understanding these dynamics is essential for effective marketing strategy.

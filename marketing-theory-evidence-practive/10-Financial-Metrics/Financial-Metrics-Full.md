# Financial Metrics

Financial metrics include items such as sales revenue, profit and cash-flow impact.

None of this is rocket science and it is taught in any basic business or accounting course. So, in this textbook we concentrate mainly on the customer-based metrics.

However, there are some important aspects about some popular financial metrics that are not always well understood by marketers—these are explained below.

---

## Profit and Profit Contribution

### Profit

**Formula:**

```
Profit = Sales Revenue – Costs
```

Profit is easily calculated for a single discrete business: it is the money left over (for owners) after all expenses are paid.

However, calculating profit for parts of the business (e.g. business units, brands or products) is more tricky. A decision has to be made regarding how much of some costs that span many parts of the business (costs such as the CEO’s salary) should be allocated to each individual part of the business.

How this is done will affect the reported profit of that part. For example:

- If an unfair amount of central costs (often called overheads) are allocated to a particular activity or brand, then it may look unprofitable.
- If too little overheads are allocated to a product, it will look artificially profitable.

Management accountants worry a good deal about getting these cost allocations right, and individual managers argue a good deal about them:

- Every brand manager wants less cost allocated to their brand.
- Every sales manager wants less cost allocated to their region.

---

### Profit Contribution

One way to side-step these arguments is to look at **profit contribution** instead.

Here, only direct costs are allocated to the brand or activity. These are the costs that are purely associated with producing and marketing that particular brand.

What’s left over (i.e. sales revenue minus direct costs) is the brand’s contribution to the firm’s central costs (and its profit).

Determining which costs are directly caused by the brand or activity can also be tricky. The important thing is to allocate costs as similarly as possible to each activity or brand so that fair comparisons can be made.

---

## Profit Margin

Profit margin is a metric that is used regularly to compare particular products.

A simple way of thinking about profit margin is that it says how much the firm earns from these sales after deducting the direct costs of manufacturing the product or providing the service. It can be summed up as:

> **“What’s in this for us.”**

Technically speaking, profit margin refers to how much contribution to fixed costs (and profits) a firm earns from its sales revenue, expressed as a percentage of that sales revenue.

For example:

- A profit margin of **20%** means:
  - **80%** of sales revenue disappears in production costs
  - **20%** remains to contribute to fixed costs and potential company profit

---

### Interpreting Changes in Profit Margins

Changes in profit margins are often seen as an indicator of the firm’s or brand’s competitiveness:

- **Eroding margins** suggest that a brand is losing competitiveness

For this reason, firms will explain to investors if there are other reasons for changes in profit margins.

#### Examples:

- **Technology companies:**

  - Profit margins may temporarily decrease when launching new technology (e.g. iPad Pro after iPad Air)
  - Over time, as production costs reduce, margins increase

- **Fashion retailers:**

  - High margins at the start of a season
  - Margins fall as products are discounted toward the end of the season

---

### Limitations of Profit Margin

Because profit margins are typically calculated without including sales and advertising costs, they don’t really tell us how profitable something is.

However, they do provide insight into:

- How much can and should be spent on marketing the product

It is important to convert percentages into actual dollar values:

- A **high margin** on a low-selling product may be worth little
- A **low margin** on a high-selling product may be worth a great deal

#### Example:

- Ferrari likely has higher profit margins per car than Toyota
- However, Toyota makes more total profit
- Toyota’s profits are several times greater than Ferrari’s entire sales revenue

---

### Discounting and Profit Margins

Profit margins also indicate how much a brand can be discounted:

- If the margin is very small:
  - There is little room for discounting
  - Risk of reaching **negative profit margin**
    - Where the brand loses more money the more it sells

## Return on Investment (ROI)

The notion of **Return on Investment (ROI)** is very popular in marketing, although the term is used very loosely. It is often used simply to mean accountability or to refer to any financial metric.

### Definition

ROI refers to the percentage return on marketing activity.

In other words:

```
ROI = (Returns − Investment) / Investment
```

---

### Example Calculation

If:

- $100,000 is invested in extra advertising
- It yields $1,110,000 in extra sales
- Which delivers $110,000 in profit contribution\
  ($1,000,000 went to costs)

Then:

```
ROI = ($110,000 − $100,000) / $100,000
```

Which becomes:

```
$10,000 / $100,000 = 10%
```

This equals:

- **10% return**
- Or **$1.10 return for every $1 invested**

---

## Problems with ROI

It sounds logical to compare campaign performance using ROI, but there are significant issues—**dangerous problems**.

### 1. Difficulty Measuring True Returns

Most marketing activity does not generate easily measurable extra sales. Instead, it:

- Maintains existing market share

Additionally:

- Many campaigns are ongoing
- It is difficult to isolate discrete investments and one-off returns

For similar reasons:

- No one calculates ROI for departments like:
  - Human resources
  - Senior management

---

### 2. ROI Focuses on Percentages, Not Dollars

ROI is a percentage (or ratio), not a dollar figure.

This leads to important implications:

- A **small campaign with high ROI** may generate less profit than:
- A **large campaign with low ROI**

#### Key Insight:

- Shareholders care about **total dollar profits**, not ROI percentages

---

### Consequences of Over-Reliance on ROI

Using ROI to compare marketing options can lead to:

- Preference for **smaller campaigns**
- Reduced overall profit

In extreme cases:

- A firm can achieve **infinite ROI** by:
  - Simply stopping advertising altogether

---

### Behavioral Biases Encouraged by ROI

ROI tends to favor:

- Campaigns with **immediate, obvious returns**
  - e.g. price discounts
- Campaigns targeting:
  - Existing, heavier buyers (who were likely to buy anyway)

This can distort marketing strategy and reduce long-term effectiveness.

# Introduction

Marketers invest significant effort, time, and resources into developing strategies to grow their brands, launch new products, and execute advertising, pricing promotions, and public relations (PR) campaigns.

Despite these efforts, marketers often expect that such investments will result in loyal, emotionally connected customers. However, the reality is quite different. Most people are too occupied with everyday life to form deep attachments to brands. Instead, they make quick decisions—often based on convenience or habit. Even brands they have previously liked can be forgotten, and consumer preferences can change rapidly.

So, what is the purpose of all this effort? Why do marketers persist?

In this chapter, you will explore key concepts such as loyalty, attitudes, memory, and motivation, along with the importance of mental and physical availability. Understanding buyer behaviour is essential for making effective marketing decisions.

---

# Knowledge is Power

As a marketer, you are expected to have a deeper understanding of your company’s customers than anyone else in the organisation. This does not mean having an in-depth psychological profile of each individual customer—that would be unrealistic. Instead, your understanding will gradually improve as you learn how various factors influence buying behaviour.

Many daily marketing decisions rely on answering fundamental questions:

* **Who buys?**
* **Where do they buy?**
* **When do they buy?**
* **How much do they buy?**

It is essential to track descriptive data about both current and potential customers, including:

* What they buy
* What they watch
* How your brand fits into their lives

However, data alone is not enough. It must be interpreted. Skilled marketers analyse data using established, law-like patterns that help identify trends and anomalies. These patterns apply across various contexts, including:

* Industrial purchases (e.g., concrete, aviation fuel)
* Retail brand purchases
* Store selection behaviour

While this chapter does not explore these patterns in detail, it focuses on what they reveal about buying behaviour and brand attitudes.

---

## The Limits of Intuition

Traditional marketers often rely on intuition and introspection, assuming their own behaviours and preferences reflect those of their customers. This approach is flawed.

Marketing professionals typically differ significantly from their customers—they are often better educated, earn higher incomes, and have a stronger emotional connection to the brand because it is tied to their livelihood. As a result, their perspective is not representative of the average consumer.

This is why **market research is critical**. Relying solely on intuition or internal opinions is insufficient (see Chapter 4 on Market Research).

---

## The Role of Market Research

Marketers benefit from a well-established market research industry, including global firms such as **Nielsen** and **Kantar**, as well as numerous specialised local agencies.

These organisations collect data through surveys that capture:

* Consumer behaviours (e.g., watching, shopping, buying, consuming)
* Consumer perceptions and opinions

Marketers must interpret this data using established knowledge of buying behaviour and human psychology. This chapter introduces some of these foundational principles.

# Most Buying is Repeat Buying

A typical supermarket carries around **40,000 products**, making even simple shopping decisions seem overwhelming. Similarly, consumers face vast choices in other areas:

* Hundreds of financial institutions
* Thousands of accountancy firms
* Numerous hairdressers
* Hundreds of television channels

Given this abundance of choice, how do consumers manage?

The answer is simple: **most buying behaviour is repetitive**.

Consumers typically:

* Purchase from categories they are already familiar with
* Choose brands they have bought before
* Shop at stores they have previously visited or that are similar

This makes consumers experienced, though not necessarily experts. Most people do not aim to become experts—they simply want to make satisfactory decisions without investing excessive time or effort.

---

## Habit and Convenience Drive Behaviour

Many marketing textbooks overemphasise rational decision-making and deliberate evaluation. While such behaviour does occur, it is relatively rare.

In reality, most purchasing decisions are driven by:

* **Habit**
* **Convenience**

Consumers develop a **repertoire**—a stable set of preferred brands or stores—and repeatedly choose from this limited selection.

## Repertoire Buying and Loyalty

This chapter explores the concept of **repertoire buying**, which can be understood as loyalty to a limited number of brands.

Key considerations include:

* How marketers can **encourage loyalty** to their brand
* How to **disrupt or influence** existing loyalties toward competing brands

Understanding these dynamics is essential for effective marketing strategy.

# High-involvement decisions

Some buying decisions have greater consequences than others, which encourages us to be more thoughtful about them. Mostly this occurs when:

* A consumer buys from a category for the first time (e.g. a new parent buys nappies)
* A consumer has an intense interest in the category (e.g. their hobby is learning about wine)
* A poor decision would have substantial consequences (e.g. buying a bottle of wine as a gift for their boss or a valuable client)

But while a few wine consumers might be highly involved in their decisions, most won’t be (or only on special occasions). So it’s more sensible to distinguish categories where many or most consumers are highly involved. Decisions that are commonly seen as being highly involving are those for expensive products, such as cars, or those with large financial consequences, such as home loans. Similarly, industrial purchases are often high-involvement; they can involve teams of people, often expert buyers, making detailed assessments of the options.

Surprisingly, research based on observation—called empirical research—shows that even in these potentially high-involvement categories, buyers do far less careful research than might be expected. Substantial evaluation appears to be more the exception rather than the rule (notice how many purchases in categories like insurance are straight-out renewals, with no evaluation of alternatives). Hence, even though some individual buyers may feel more involved (and stressed by the purchase), together their actual brand-buying behaviours show the same repeat-buying patterns as in low-involvement buying.

For example, repeat-purchasing levels for car brands are quite high: near to 50 per cent repeat levels (with some variation between countries). In other words, almost half the time new car buyers buy the same brand as they bought last time (Ehrenberg & Bound, 1999). This level of repeat-buying seems incredible considering that in modern market economies there are typically 60 or so brands to choose from.

Also, usually a number of years pass between purchases, models have changed, as have buyers’ life circumstances and needs. The odds of someone choosing the same car brand again appear to be very slim. If buying were random, there would be less than one chance in 50 of a person purchasing the same brand again. But buying is not random; there is clear loyalty.

The 50 per cent retention rate for car brands occurs because buyers consider only around two brands on average, one of which is usually the brand they bought last time—which is the brand they drive now (Lambert-Pandraud, Laurent & Lapersonne, 2005). About a fifth of buyers consider only a single brand, which is almost always the one they bought last time (Lapersonne, Laurent & Le Goff, 1995).

So this ‘high-involvement’ purchase decision doesn’t seem to lead to many buyers evaluating any more than a few brands. Very similar limited search behaviour has been recorded for retail banking services and insurance, either for new purchases or repeats (Dawes, Mundt & Sharp, 2009), with even higher rates of repeat purchase.

The internet might have radically changed consumer behaviour by facilitating information search and consideration of competitive brands; however, research has found very limited online search behaviour. For example, Johnson and colleagues (2004) investigated the extent of web-based information searches. Using panel data from over 10,000 internet households, they found that on average households visit only:

* 1.2 book sites
* 1.3 CD sites
* 1.8 travel sites

during a typical active month in each category.

There are times when consumers spend more time on evaluation and active search (hence the value of online ‘search advertising’—see Chapter 12); however, in the main they make heavy use of their memories, and are happy to buy based on existing habits. To paraphrase Roberts and Nedungadi (1995: 5): there are many brands consumers would consider if only they had thought of them.

# Natural loyalty

The !Kung San of the Kalahari Desert in southern Africa believe that babies must be taught to sit, that it does not come naturally. They prop piles of dirt around babies to push them into a sitting position. Other cultures find this amusing; similarly, many cultures find it equally amusing when Westerners talk to babies, often in ‘baby talk’, in the belief that babies can somehow understand such sentences and must be spoken to in order to stimulate their own speaking.

Harvard professor and cognitive scientist Stephen Pinker (1994) points out that learning language, like learning to sit, comes naturally—we have a natural disposition (presumably underpinned by genetics).

Consumer loyalty may also be a natural phenomenon. Consumers’ loyalty to brands has traditionally been portrayed as an outcome of marketing strategy. The traditional thinking is that it is something that must be earned (or bought) and will erode if a brand slips in what makes it different—called differentiation—value or service quality.

However, more recently we have come to realise that this is an exaggeration; this belief ignores how loyalty comes easily to consumers. The first clue that loyalty is a strong natural tendency is that loyalty is very prevalent. Consumers seldom ever buy randomly, but instead show biased purchasing, favouring some brands over others.

From soap purchasing to soap opera viewing, consumers show biased non-random repeat-purchasing—in other words, each consumer has their own personal loyalties.

---

# Loyalty is everywhere

We observe loyal behaviour in all categories, including service purchasing and corporate buying. Buyers restrict their purchases to a personal repertoire of brands—people buy far fewer different brands than they could.

Ten purchases from the category usually means far fewer than ten different brands are bought—buyers keep returning to their “favourites”. And this set can be very small.

> **Note:** We use the term *favourites* with some caution, as it implies that buyers have strong attitudinal preferences, which usually isn’t the case. All we mean by this term is that consumers have brands that they each buy more often—and repeat-purchasing is biased towards these brands.

Here’s another surprising example of how loyal we are. Television channel choice is somewhere where loyalty is rather unexpected; most of us think we watch television for the programs and—aside from the genre-specific channels (e.g. the weather channel)—we have only a vague idea of what channel offers which of our favourite programs.

Also, there are no real switching costs: we can defect from one channel to another at the push of a button. In spite of all of this, television channel choice reveals the same pattern as other categories—people substantially restrict their repertoires and show loyalty.

Figure 2.1 was compiled by Nielsen researchers using Nielsen’s National TV Panel (2016). It shows:

* The number of television channels each household has access to
* How many they actually watch each week

A clear pattern in this data can be seen:

* Households with access to 40 channels watch about a dozen each week
* Households with access to 80 channels watch a few more than a dozen
* Households with access to 101+ channels still only watch about 20

What Figure 2.1 shows is that viewers keep returning to their favourite channels. There is obvious loyalty. Most viewers watch enough television to watch many more channels than they do, but they prefer to restrict their personal viewing repertoire.

This figure would look almost identical if it concerned brand buying in any product category—it could be used to show the number of purchases made by an individual and how many different brands they had bought. Loyalty is everywhere, it seems.

Brand loyalty is part of every market, even in so-called ‘commodity’ categories (such as rice or flour). It is a sensible buyer strategy, since this tendency to be loyal is useful to human beings in balancing risk and saving the precious commodity of time.

# Why loyalty programs don’t work

Consumers’ natural tendency to be loyal is one of the reasons why loyalty programs do so little to improve brand loyalty (see Sharp & Sharp, 1997).

Unfortunately for company profitability, it’s also the customers who are already the most loyal who join the company’s loyalty program:

* They are more likely to hear of the program since they shop there more often
* The program is much more attractive to them

These customers then largely receive rewards for doing what they were already doing.

## Loyalty without differentiation

It’s common to think that brand differentiation is essential for loyalty—that consumers cannot behaviourally show loyalty unless there are meaningful differences between brands. This is the notion that loyalty occurs because each consumer finds the brand that suits their needs best and so then buys this rather than the other options.

This all sounds very sensible, but it turns out to be an exaggeration.

Similar examples of behavioural loyalties have been noted and studied, for example:

* Choice of seat in lecture theatres
* Computer terminals in computer labs
* Even toilet roll orientation (Figure 2.2)

There is a Facebook page dedicated to those who say they must always have the television volume on an even number. Another page exists for people who use all the same coloured pegs when hanging out washing.

Consumers commonly cite ‘habit’ as the reason for such loyalties—or that ‘it makes life easier’—but it is also common for these behavioural loyalties to subsequently lead to attitudinal preferences.

For instance, talkback radio stations find no shortage of people to take sides in debates about which way is the correct or best orientation for toilet paper rolls.

# Experimental evidence of natural loyalty

The natural tendency towards brand loyalty is revealed in experiments that show how rapidly consumers form loyalties—even when choosing between identical products.

### Bread experiment (Tucker, 1964)

* Loaves of bread from the same commercial oven were offered
* 42 different households participated each day for 12 days
* Loaves were wrapped identically
* Each day, loaves were labelled differently (e.g. ‘L’, ‘M’, ‘P’, ‘H’)
* Positions of the ‘brands’ were rotated

**Findings:**

* Many respondents quickly adopted loyalties
* Each household favoured a particular ‘brand’
* Some also favoured positions on the tray

> “It is clear that such loyalties are more than trivial, even though they are based on what may seem trivial distinctions” (Tucker, 1964: 35)

---

### Beer experiment (McConnell, 1968)

McConnell replicated and extended this experiment using bottles of identical beer.

**Findings:**

* Consumers quickly formed personal loyalties
* Some households favoured brand M, others brand L, etc.

### Re-analysis (Charlton & Ehrenberg, 1973)

Charlton and Ehrenberg re-analysed McConnell’s data and confirmed:

* Loyalty patterns matched real-world repeat-buying behaviour
* This occurred despite:

  * No product differentiation
  * No packaging differences
  * No advertising
  * No differences in physical availability

**Conclusion:**
Loyalty needs little prompting—it comes naturally.

# Brand performance and loyalty

Marketers use statistics called *brand performance metrics* to measure the performance of their brand.

The pattern of individual loyalty underpins regularities in brand performance metrics in many, if not all, markets (Ehrenberg, Uncles & Goodhardt, 2004).

This is evidenced by the empirical **double jeopardy law**, which shows:

* Brands with large and small market shares differ substantially in how many people buy them
* But differ far less in terms of how loyal their buyers are

(Ehrenberg & Goodhardt, 2002)

Ultimately, every brand gets its share of loyal customers—seldom more or less than others.

**Conclusion:**
Consumer loyalty has more to do with consumers themselves than with specific features of the brand.

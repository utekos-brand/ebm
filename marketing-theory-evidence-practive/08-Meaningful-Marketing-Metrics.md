# Meaningful Marketing Metrics

## Introduction

Just as doctors need measures to assess the health of their patients, marketing managers need measures to assess the health of their brands and to judge the effects of their marketing campaigns. Sales revenue and contribution to profit are vital metrics—but they aren’t enough.

They can show a decline or an increase, but more metrics are needed to help explain why this happened. A thorough metrics system includes:

* Financial metrics
* Buying behaviour metrics
* Memory metrics
* Customer profile metrics
* Marketing activity metrics

In order to interpret any metric correctly, marketers need to be aware of benchmarks and expected patterns—in other words, is the score high, low or as it should be?

---

## Why Marketers Need Metrics

If you aren’t measuring something, even in a rough way, then you can’t be managing it.

Chefs look at, smell and taste their food—usually as they cook, not just afterwards. They do so in order to assess if their actions are making the food hotter, sweeter, saltier, tastier and so on.

Similarly, marketing managers need measurement to assess and guide their marketing actions. Marketing metrics:

* Show how the brand and business is performing
* Provide diagnostic information on how to improve

Without a comprehensive set of meaningful metrics, a marketing director simply cannot tell if they are doing a good job or not.

Contrary to popular belief, you don’t have to be a maths geek to be good at interpreting quantitative data. In this chapter, we’ll show how:

* A sound understanding of what metrics mean
* Combined with simple rules of data presentation

can give a marketer real advantages:

* The ability to see meaning in data where others can’t
* The ability to present persuasive data-driven arguments

## Are Metrics Meaningful?

Increasing amounts of money and management attention are being spent on marketing metrics. Marketers are demanding metrics for:

* Management purposes
* Reporting performance to company boards
* Shareholders
* External stakeholders

For example, companies now report metrics on their environmental record.

Research agencies, media companies and consultants are continuously introducing new metrics. Yet many of these metrics are misleading or unhelpful.

Marketing academic John Bound once described his long career as a market research manager in large corporations (before joining academia) as excelling in wasting his company’s money—with a note that he was sure that people do every bit as good a job today as he did then.

Most market research is technically done very well, but it often fails to produce useful, meaningful marketing metrics. Problems include:

* Measuring the wrong things
* Measuring in the wrong ways
* Misinterpreting results

---

## When Metrics Are Not Useful

### Metrics That Do Not Change

There is no point in tracking and regularly reporting metrics that:

* Do not change
* Change very slowly

A good, well-funded medical practice will measure:

* Weight and blood pressure at each visit
* Height of adult patients only rarely

Many marketing metrics such as customer satisfaction and brand image are measured and reported far too frequently.

Fortunately for market researchers, random sampling variation makes figures fluctuate slightly from survey to survey, creating the illusion of change. This leads to:

* Misinterpretation
* Wasted management time
* Incorrect explanations

### Metrics That Are Predictable

There is little point in tracking metrics that are already perfectly predictable from other metrics.

Example:

* Medical practitioners do not test male patients for pregnancy

Similarly, many marketing metrics are predictable from:

* Market share
* Market penetration (number of customers)

These metrics:

* Are always higher for brands with larger market share
* Often duplicate information

Much market research effectively asks respondents, in different ways, how often they buy the brand (see ‘Marketing’s attitude problem’ on page 65).

---

### Metrics Without Meaning

There is little point in tracking metrics when you do not know:

* What they mean
* How they relate to other metrics
* How they relate to marketing actions
* What level they should be

Many proprietary metrics fall into this category.

Just because a measure has an impressive name (e.g. *brand equity*, *brand health*) does not mean it is meaningful.

## The Importance of Interpretation

Imagine if your doctor measured your blood pressure and, when asked why, replied:

> “No idea … we were taught how to take blood pressure at medical school, so I do it for all my patients—always have!”

A blood pressure reading (e.g. 140 over 90) is meaningless on its own. Questions arise:

* Is it high, low or normal?
* Why does it matter?

Even if repeated measurements confirm reliability, the data remains meaningless without context.

---

## Turning Data into Information

To interpret data, doctors rely on scientific knowledge, including:

* Relationships between measurements
* Differences between healthy and unhealthy individuals
* Variations by age, gender and medication

With this knowledge, a doctor can determine that 140 over 90 is high and understand its implications.

Marketing professionals also need scientific knowledge to:

* Make metrics meaningful
* Improve management decisions

Marketing research managers need this knowledge to avoid wasting money on ineffective research.

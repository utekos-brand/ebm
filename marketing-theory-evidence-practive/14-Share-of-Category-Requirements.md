# Share of Category Requirements (SCR)

How many of all your petrol purchases do you make at BP, and how many at Shell?

How many of your supermarket shopping trips are to Coles, and how often do you go to Woolworths?

The loyalty metric **share of category requirements (SCR)** refers to the proportion of category purchases that go to a particular brand.

* If a consumer went supermarket shopping 10 times during a period
* And for five of those times they shopped at Woolworths

Then they devoted **50% SCR** to Woolworths.

Depending on the industry, this metric can also be referred to as:

* **Share of wallet** (financial services)
* **Share of mouth** (e.g. drinks)

SCR is always expressed as a percentage ranging from **0% to 100%**.

---

## Brand-Level SCR

When reported at the brand level, SCR refers to the **average SCR of all purchasers** of the brand during the period.

* Non-buyers are **not included** in the calculation
* Example:

  * Buyer 1: 40% SCR
  * Buyer 2: 80% SCR
  * Brand SCR = **60%**

---

## Example: Nescafé

Continuing with the example in Table 3.3:

* Nescafé is the **leading market share brand**
* It also has the **highest SCR (37%)**

This means:

* Customers who bought Nescafé satisfied **37% of their instant coffee needs** with it
* Some customers had **100% SCR** (only bought Nescafé)
* Others bought mostly other brands

The average across all buyers results in **37% SCR**.

---

## Double Jeopardy Pattern

SCR follows the **double jeopardy law**:

* Larger brands → **higher SCR**
* Smaller brands → **lower SCR**

---

## Time Dependency of SCR

SCR is **time-dependent**, meaning:

* The time period must always be specified
* Over longer periods:

  * SCR **decreases**
  * Consumers develop **larger repertoires**

However:

* The decline is **not linear**
* Doubling time does **not halve SCR**
* Over time, SCR **levels off**

This happens because:

* Consumers repeatedly buy brands they already know
* Eventually, their full repertoire is revealed
* Additional time rarely introduces new brands

---

## Cross-Selling and Related Metrics

> “There is little difference in cross-selling metrics between competing brands, and the small differences that do exist tend to reflect market share—not whether or not they have dedicated cross-selling programs.”
> — Sharp (2010), *How Brands Grow*, Oxford University Press

A related metric is:

### Number of Products per Customer

Example:

* A bank customer may have:

  * Credit card
  * Home loan
  * Home insurance

This metric is used to measure:

* **Loyalty**
* **Depth of relationship**
* **Cross-selling success**

### Key Insights

* Cross-selling metrics vary **little between brands**
* Improvements usually occur only with **increased market share**

---

## Wells Fargo Case Study

For nearly two decades, Wells Fargo claimed:

* Competitors: ~2 products per customer
* Wells Fargo: ~5 products per customer
* Target: 8 products per customer

### Research Findings

* Cross-selling metrics follow **double jeopardy**
* Differences between firms are **minimal**

### Scandal (2016)

* 5,300 employees fired
* Over **2.5 million fake accounts** created
* Metrics improved artificially, but:

  * No real revenue increase

### Consequences

* CEO resigned
* $185 million fine
* $5 million refunded to customers

### Key Lesson

Understanding marketing metrics can prevent:

* Setting **unrealistic targets**
* Being misled by **false performance indicators**

# Solely (100%) Loyal Customers

Can you think of a category where you only ever buy one brand?

## Definition

The proportion of **solely loyal customers** refers to:

* Buyers who purchased **only one brand**
* Equivalent to **100% SCR**

---

## Reality of Sole Loyalty

Although appealing:

* Most customers **buy multiple brands**
* Solely loyal customers are **rare**

Example:

* Instant coffee brands:

  * Around **11%** of buyers are solely loyal annually

---

## Subscription Markets

Higher sole loyalty occurs in:

* Mortgages
* Mobile contracts

However:

* When viewed broadly (e.g. banking), these still behave like **repertoire markets**

---

## Time Effects

* Short time periods:

  * More 100% loyal customers
  * Fewer purchase opportunities

* Long time periods:

  * Fewer 100% loyal customers
  * More brand switching

---

## Buyer Behavior

Solely loyal customers tend to be:

* **Light buyers**

Reason:

* More purchases = more chances to try other brands

---

## Double Jeopardy Effect

* Larger brands → **more 100% loyal customers**
* Smaller brands → **fewer**

---

# Defection Rate

## Definition

Defection rate is:

* The proportion of past customers who have **stopped buying the brand**

---

## Measurement

Easier in industries like:

* Finance (account closure or inactivity)

Also measurable via:

* Surveys asking about discontinued brand usage

---

## Interpretation

Customer loss may seem alarming, but:

* Nearly **half of defections** are unrelated to the brand

Reasons include:

* No longer needing the category
* Life changes
* Taste changes

---

## Key Insights

* Even successful brands **lose customers**
* Zero defection is **not possible**

---

## Double Jeopardy and Defection

* Larger brands → **lower defection rates**
* Smaller brands → **higher defection rates**

---

## Growth vs Decline

Research shows:

* Declining brands:

  * Not high defection
  * But **low acquisition**

* Growing brands:

  * Not low defection
  * But **strong acquisition**

---

## Conclusion

The key driver of growth is:

* **Customer acquisition**, not eliminating defection

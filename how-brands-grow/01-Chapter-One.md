# How Brands Grow

- Second edition: Revised

## Chapter 1: How Brands Grow

- Byron Sharp and Jenni Romaniuk

This chapter examines whether sales growth is primarily driven by penetration (increasing the number of customers) or loyalty (increasing purchase frequency among existing customers). It documents the law of double jeopardy across multiple contexts:

- emerging markets
- services
- durables
- local and global brands
- a wide range of loyalty metrics

The chapter explains the mechanisms behind double jeopardy and specifies the conditions under which brands conform to or deviate from this law.

### How to grow

Marketing involves meeting customer needs. Growth requires serving more customers. In competitive markets, growth is necessary because competitors attempt to capture the same customers. Increasing market share enables scale, supports competitive marketing budgets, and provides justification for intermediaries to distribute the brand.

Managers of small brands may allocate resources to ineffective activities or introduce unnecessary changes that damage the brand. Managers of large brands may rely on strong performance metrics and fail to respond to competitive threats. This book identifies effective strategies and common risks.

This chapter presents evidence demonstrating that increasing penetration is the primary driver of growth. Loyalty metrics are largely predictable based on penetration levels. This pattern applies across emerging markets, services, durable goods, and packaged goods in developed markets.

### Can you engineer your brand loyalty?

A central marketing strategy question is whether to prioritize increasing loyalty among existing customers or acquiring new customers. Both approaches can generate growth, but their relative effectiveness differs.

- The loyalty approach increases revenue by encouraging existing customers to purchase more frequently or reduce purchases from competitors. This approach may involve improving brand attractiveness through service enhancements, loyalty programs (e.g., incentives or points), or cross-selling related products or services. For example, personal insurance providers often cross-sell home insurance to car insurance customers and vice versa.

  Loyalty strategies are assumed to reduce marketing costs by targeting a smaller audience. Existing customers are considered easier to reach and convert compared to non-buyers. Therefore, additional sales from existing customers are assumed to have lower acquisition costs.

  In service categories, retention strategies aim to reduce customer defections. This approach assumes that retaining customers increases the customer base indirectly through positive word of mouth. Retained customers are expected to attract new customers while maintaining existing relationships.

- Empirical evidence contradicts these assumptions. How Brands Grow (2010) presents extensive data showing that growth results from increases in both penetration and loyalty, but the majority of growth comes from increased penetration. Loyalty improvements are typically a consequence of increased penetration rather than an independent driver.

  A strategy focused primarily on loyalty does not produce significant growth. Higher loyalty levels occur only when penetration increases substantially. Despite this evidence, some marketing consultants and academics continue to promote loyalty-focused strategies.

  Marketing operates within observable patterns similar to physical laws. Engineers design within the constraints of physics; marketers operate within the constraints of competitive market behavior. Strategic decisions regarding media, targeting, pricing, and distribution must align with these patterns. Successful strategies produce consistent outcomes: as a brand grows, both penetration and loyalty increase in predictable proportions relative to market share. This pattern is defined as the law of double jeopardy.

### The Law of Double Jeopardy

Double jeopardy was identified at the NBC television network and later formalized by William McPhee (McPhee, 1963). His work, _Formal Theories of Mass Behaviour_, documented that less well-known radio announcers (first jeopardy) are also less liked by those who are aware of them (second jeopardy). Andrew Ehrenberg (1972) and Claude Martin (1973) independently identified the same pattern in brand choice: smaller brands have fewer customers (first jeopardy) and slightly lower loyalty among those customers (second jeopardy).

The law of double jeopardy has been observed across multiple domains, including industrial brands, services, retail stores, store chains, comic strips, newspapers, radio stations, television networks, television programs, and political candidates. Recent studies confirm its presence in fruit and vegetable purchasing, political polling, and Twitter usage (Graham et al., 2017; Kooyman & Wright, 2017; Rogers et al., 2017). A brand could theoretically achieve similar sales to a rival brand while maintaining a smaller but more loyal customer base (a niche brand). This outcome is rare. Small market-share brands typically exhibit penetration and loyalty metrics consistent with their size. Even brands that display niche characteristics rarely achieve loyalty levels comparable to larger brands.

Brands with similar market share typically exhibit:

- Similar penetration levels (number of buyers within a defined period)
- Similar loyalty levels (purchase frequency, number of products held, share of category buying, repeat-buy rates)

Loyalty metrics vary by category, but the double jeopardy pattern remains consistent regardless of the metric used.

Table 1.2 also contradicts the claim that Chinese consumers lack brand loyalty and primarily purchase based on price (Lavin, 2018). His research identified double jeopardy patterns, where smaller retailers and brands exhibit lower penetration and loyalty compared to larger brands (Uncles, 2010; Uncles & Kwok, 2008). Bennett (2008) reported similar findings in the durable goods sector, specifically television purchases in China. These patterns persist in electronics categories.

### How Brands Change in Penetration and Loyalty Over Time

Brand growth or decline follows the double jeopardy pattern. Changes in market share correspond to changes in penetration, with loyalty metrics adjusting accordingly. A single time-point measurement does not indicate growth or decline.

Research examining deviations from double jeopardy shows no predictive value for future brand performance (Kearns, Millar & Lewis, 2000). Brands do not initiate growth through elevated loyalty relative to their size, nor do they decline due to reduced loyalty prior to loss of share.

Barker (2021) identifies that small brands are more likely to grow when moving from below-expected loyalty to expected loyalty levels, and more likely to decline when moving from expected to below-expected loyalty. Penetration remains the primary driver of market share change.

Evidence shows that changes in brand size are primarily driven by customer acquisition levels. Both growing and declining brands lose customers at rates consistent with their market share. Growth occurs when acquisition exceeds expected levels; decline occurs when acquisition falls below expected levels (Riebe et al., 2014). The decline of US automotive brands relative to Japanese and Korean competitors resulted from reduced acquisition, not increased defection (Sharp, 2009).

### Penetration Rules

Market share changes primarily reflect changes in penetration, with smaller corresponding changes in loyalty metrics. Studies of incremental market share changes (e.g., 0.3 or 0.5 percentage points annually) show that penetration accounts for most variation (Anschuetz, 2002; Baldinger, Blair & Echambadi, 2002; McDonald & Ehrenberg, 2003; Nenycz-Thiel, Dawes & Romaniuk, 2018; Romaniuk, Dawes & Nenycz-Thiel, 2014; Sylvester, McQueen & Moore, 1994).

Most brands operate below high penetration thresholds (e.g., below 70% annual penetration). Exceptions to double jeopardy occur when brands have extremely high penetration or operate within constrained niches (e.g., limited geographic distribution). In such cases, growth or decline is reflected more in loyalty metrics due to limited potential for penetration expansion.

Niche brands have restricted growth potential. Expansion requires addressing structural limitations such as distribution. Small brands without such constraints retain growth potential.

Most brands occupy positions along the lower to middle range of the double jeopardy distribution (Allsopp, Sharp & Dawes, 2004; Ehrenberg, Goodhardt & Barwise, 1990; Uncles et al., 1994). Movement along this distribution typically involves larger changes in penetration and smaller changes in loyalty.

### Why Double Jeopardy Occurs

In competitive markets with substitutable options, brands differ in:

- Mental availability: likelihood of being recalled in buying situations
- Physical availability: ease of purchase and accessibility

Large brands exhibit high mental and physical availability, resulting in higher penetration. Small brands exhibit lower availability, resulting in lower penetration.

Loyalty differences align with brand size due to the same availability factors. Large brands are considered across more purchase occasions and are available in more locations, including situations with limited alternatives. Light category buyers are more likely to know and purchase larger brands.

Small brands have lower awareness and are known primarily by heavier category buyers, who also purchase multiple brands, including larger competitors.

Large popular brands have excellent mental and physical availability amongst the buying population. This means more people will buy them (higher penetration). Small brands will have far less mental and physical availability. Indeed some people don’t even know these brands exist, and many potential buyers seldom, if ever, notice them. Therefore fewer people buy them (lower penetration) in any time period.
But why do brands also vary in their loyalty scores, and vary systematically in line with their size? Again, the answer lies in mental and physical availability and the fact that these combine to be the dominant driver of a brand’s market share. It means that very popular brands are thought of by more people across more category-buying occasions and are available to buy in some places where they are the sole brand (or one of very few). Similarly, many light or infrequent buyers of the category don’t know of many brands, and the few brands these buyers do know are much more likely to be the more popular brands.
Small brands unenviably find themselves in the opposite position. Fewer people know them and fewer know them well, while the people who do know them tend to be heavier category buyers—and these people also buy many other brands, including the big brands. Small brands are available in fewer outlets and these tend to be common locations where they are displayed alongside many other (larger) competitors.
The higher competition faced by small brands is illustrated in Figure 1.2 for fast food brands in Russia and South Korea. In both cases, a small brand’s customers buy many more other brands than the customers of big brands do. For example, in Russia, customers of the biggest brand, McDonald’s, also buy from 2.6 other quick-service food brands; while in the same time period, customers of the much smaller Sbarro’s buy from more than twice as many brands (5.6). This law-like pattern is known as the natural monopoly law, a title that refers to the fact that larger share brands have a greater proportion of their customer base made up of light category buyers, and therefore monopolise the light category buyers (also see Dawes 2020).
These differences result in customers of smaller brands being aware of and purchasing a wider range of options. When a small brand is tried and liked, competitive conditions reduce both mental availability (ease of recall) and physical availability (ease of purchase). In contrast, a brand such as McDonald’s maintains high mental and physical availability. Even after a negative experience, repeat purchase remains likely due to ease of access and recall.

If availability (mental and physical) is the primary driver of brand choice, then the double jeopardy pattern applies: brands with higher penetration also exhibit slightly higher loyalty metrics.

In the Indonesian banking example in Table 1.4, Bank Tabungan Negara (BTN) shows a higher defection rate than other listed banks. This is not attributable to customer dissatisfaction or poor service. It results from two factors:

• A limited product range focused on subsidised housing loans rather than a broader transaction account offering, which reduces mental availability.

• A smaller branch network (approximately 100 branches) compared to Bank Mandiri (approximately 2000 branches) and Bank Rakyat Indonesia (approximately 4000 branches), which reduces physical availability.

In financial services, defection is often driven by changes in customer circumstances, such as relocation, marriage, or family expansion. Smaller brands with limited locations, fewer branches, and less recognised product ranges are more exposed to these changes, resulting in higher defection rates and lower loyalty.

Brand loyalty correlates with penetration. Both are determined by mental and physical availability. The double jeopardy pattern would not occur if brands were strongly differentiated and appealed exclusively to specific customer segments.

Truong (2014) analysed double jeopardy deviations across six emerging markets (Brazil, China, India, Indonesia, Malaysia, and the Philippines). Across more than 450 brands in twenty repeat-purchase categories, only 10% deviated significantly from expected loyalty levels. No systematic deviations were identified across the following brand types:

- Local brands
- Global brands
- Higher-priced brands
- Discount brands
- Country of origin

Approximately half of the deviations involved dominant market leaders exhibiting slightly higher-than-expected loyalty, consistent with observations in developed markets (Fader & Schmittlein, 1993; Pare & Dawes, 2011). The remaining deviations were evenly split between higher and lower than expected loyalty, with no consistent pattern or explanation.

Deviations from double jeopardy are uncommon and typically minor. For example, in emerging markets, local brands often operate regionally and lack nationwide distribution. This limits penetration, causing loyalty metrics to appear high relative to penetration. This effect is similar to store brands distributed within a single retail chain and is often misinterpreted as strong loyalty.

In rare cases, brands exhibit lower loyalty than expected for their penetration level. This typically occurs when purchases are tied to specific occasions, needs, or seasonal factors. This indicates a category definition issue. For example, premium whisky brands, often purchased for special occasions or as gifts, appear atypical when analysed alongside standard whisky brands. When analysed within the premium segment, their metrics align with expectations.

The conditions required for systematic deviation from double jeopardy—high differentiation and strong segment-specific appeal—are commonly assumed in marketing theory but are not supported by empirical evidence. Double jeopardy is consistently observed across markets. Differentiation is generally limited and has less impact than assumed (see _How Brands Grow_). Substantial deviations are rare and typically have clear explanations.

Double jeopardy applies across most markets and categories, including emerging markets, services, durable goods, and disrupted sectors such as transport and accommodation. Even in categories with functional differences between brands, deviations remain minimal.

For example, 1st for Women insurance in South Africa targets female customers, with 90% of its customer base being female compared to approximately 50% for other brands. Despite this atypical positioning, its loyalty, measured as the number of products held, is 1.9. This is only slightly higher than expected given its low penetration (see Table 1.6). The marginal increase reflects its relative size within a smaller segment.

### (Back to) How to grow

Double jeopardy defines a growth requirement: increasing market share requires increasing the customer base. This requires reaching category buyers who rarely or never purchase the brand. Focusing on existing customers, particularly heavy buyers, does not generate substantial growth. This approach may reduce growth by neglecting potential customers and failing to replace lost buyers. Further discussion appears in Chapter 2.

In emerging markets, focusing only on current customers ignores opportunities created by category expansion. New category buyers must be acquired to benefit from growth. A brand with a 10% market share must acquire 10% of new category entrants to maintain its share.

If new customers are acquired, and the product meets functional requirements, and mental and physical availability are maintained, repeat purchase will occur. Loyalty results from effective marketing that increases penetration. Increased effectiveness in customer acquisition leads to increased loyalty.

### Dismantling barriers to market-share growth

An evidence-based growth strategy requires removal of internal barriers. Growth requires a focus on market penetration. Marketing activities targeting new customers also reach existing customers, who are more likely to notice brand communications (Harrison, 2013; Vaughan, Beal & Romaniuk, 2016). Targeting non-customers and light customers does not exclude existing customers. Strategies focused only on existing customers exclude non-buyers.

Avoid strategies that prioritise loyalty initiatives targeting existing or heavy customers. In developed markets, loyalty programs and customer relationship management (CRM) systems were promoted as methods to reduce defection and increase cross-selling. Empirical evidence shows that cross-selling metrics continue to follow the double jeopardy pattern regardless of CRM adoption or customer satisfaction levels.

For example, banks show minimal variation in the number of products held per customer. Larger banks with higher penetration exhibit slightly higher cross-product loyalty, consistent with double jeopardy.

Growth barriers often originate within the marketing strategy. Additional barriers include impediments to purchase. Evaluate whether the brand is difficult to buy or use, lacks sufficient product variants, or is priced above mainstream affordability.

In the United States, large housing and storage capacity enable bulk purchasing. In emerging markets, limited storage capacity restricts this behaviour, creating barriers to penetration. In Thailand, premium Western haircare brands were desirable but packaged in sizes exceeding typical weekly budgets. Companies such as L’Oréal, Unilever, and Procter & Gamble introduced smaller package sizes. Although smaller packages increase price per unit, they reduce the total purchase cost, lowering the entry barrier. This enables occasional purchase by a broader audience and more frequent purchase by price-sensitive segments such as teenagers.

### Meaningful loyalty metrics

Double jeopardy provides context for interpreting loyalty metrics. It enables prediction of changes in loyalty and penetration as a brand grows. It also supports identification of atypical brands, such as niche or change-of-pace brands. If deviations cannot be corrected, investment decisions should reflect limited growth potential.

### Conclusion

The double jeopardy law applies across emerging and developed markets, services and products, consumables and durables. It has remained consistent over time, including during market globalisation and the expansion of digital and mobile channels. It applies even in regulated sectors such as financial services.

Brand growth requires increased market penetration through expanded mental and physical availability. Excessive focus on loyalty, either by attempting to increase it directly or by assuming it does not exist and relying on price promotions or incentives, is inefficient.

Do not assume that brand-specific characteristics produce a loyalty advantage. If a brand deviates from double jeopardy, identify barriers to penetration, such as limited distribution. These barriers may inflate perceived loyalty and restrict growth.

Double jeopardy indicates that growth depends on reaching non-customers and light customers. Chapter 2 examines these buyers, their strategic importance, and the challenges associated with influencing low-frequency purchasers.
